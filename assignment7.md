# Detention inspection scores fail to reflect spike of deaths in ICE custody 

Improvements in Immigration and Customs Enforcement’s detention facility inspection scores in 2025 contradict the drastic surge of deaths in ICE custody.

The Office of Detention Oversight within ICE is in charge of conducting yearly inspections into critical conditions at ICE detention facilities, checking that the facilities are abiding by National Detention Standards. These standards shape fundamental health and safety conditions for the people detained in facilities that detain immigrants worldwide.   

Inspections count violations of detention standards as “deficiencies” to calculate a final facility rating: Superior, Good, Acceptable/Adequate, or Failure.

If a detention facility fails two consecutive detention inspections, they can lose federal funding.

An analysis conducted by the Project on Government Oversight found that from fiscal years 2024 to 2025 ODO gave 22 more Superior ratings to detention facilities: a 67% increase.

Facility failure ratings dropped as well — in 2024, there were 8 failures while 2025 only saw 1 failure.

Despite the improving inspection facility ratings, deaths in ICE custody continue to increase. 2025 was ICE’s deadliest year in two decades, in which 32 people died in ICE custody, according to an article published by The Guardian.

**Note about data collection process**

Because I extracted this story from data that I collected for my internship at the Investigative Reporting Workshop, I cannot yet share the official spreadsheet. However, I can tell you my methodology. I collected the facility ratings from ODO inspection reports for every facility from Fiscal Year 2022 to 2026.

I calculated the number of Superior, Good, Acceptable/Adequate, and Failure ratings for each Fiscal Year, and calculated the percent change over time for ratings from 2024 to 2025.

I have not collected more data outside of the 2022 to 2026 fiscal years because facility ratings do not exist before FY 2022. ICE records falling and passing grades within [FY Detention Statistics] (https://www.ice.gov/detain/detention-management) from FY 2019 to FY 2026, but the data purposefully excludes detention facilities that detain fewer than one person on average per day.
